Discrepancies in public data publications of the EU states

 

ODIN Score

The importance of openness of data has increased in recent years. So for example, in 2019, the old EU Open Data Directive of 2003 has been updated to improve the openness of its data. The directive also addresses data quality, but tends not to put it on an equal footing with openness, as the focus is clearly on improving the openness point.
Nevertheless, the coverage or quality of the published data is also a necessary requirement, since without qualitative data, the best openness is worthless, because even if everyone is free to use the data, the problems cannot be solved without sufficient data. What is openness if there is no data that can be open? This interactive website shows the discrepancy between openness and coverage and the gaps in coverage in public released data of the EU countries. For this, the ODIN Score collected by Open Data Watch is visualized. The goal of Open Data Watch is "to help countries build better and more resilient national statistical systems". The Odin Score evaluates the governmental statistical publications and rates each country with a score between 0 and 100.

Discrepancy between openness and coverage

The Odin score is calculated by combining the openness score and the coverage score. The openness here generally refers to the accessibility of the data and the coverage is the amount and detail of the published data.
Worldwide more than half of the EU countries are among the top 30 in the overall score. Nevertheless, the current trend is that generally more attention is paid to openness than to coverage. This is evident in the comparison of the openness and coverage scores of the respective countries.

Gaps in Coverage Score

The coverage score can be divided into 22 data categories from the fields of economic statistics, social statistics and environmental statistics. Each data category gets its own coverage score between 0 and 100 which is based on the coverage or amount and detail of data in the respective category.

Gaps in coverage of data categories

The coverage score of each data category can be divided into 5 values, the criteria of the coverage score. The first value describes the indicators and disaggregations, that means how many gradations were made in the statistics. The availability from the last 5 and 10 years describes in each case whether or how many data are available from the corresponding period. The administrative levels describe the amount of data that was not collected nationwide, but in the respective administrative levels.
